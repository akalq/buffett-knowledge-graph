**BERKSHIRE HATHAWAY INC.**\n\n```\n March 3, 1983\n\nTo the Stockholders of Berkshire Hathaway Inc.:\n\n Operating earnings of $31.5 million in 1982 amounted to only \n9.8% of beginning equity capital (valuing securities at cost), \ndown from 15.2% in 1981 and far below our recent high of 19.4% in \n1978. This decline largely resulted from:\n\n (1) a significant deterioration in insurance underwriting \n results;\n\n (2) a considerable expansion of equity capital without a \n corresponding growth in the businesses we operate \n directly; and\n\n (3) a continually-enlarging commitment of our resources to \n investment in partially-owned, nonoperated businesses; \n accounting rules dictate that a major part of our \n pro-rata share of earnings from such businesses must be \n excluded from Berkshire’s reported earnings.\n\n It was only a few years ago that we told you that the \noperating earnings/equity capital percentage, with proper \nallowance for a few other variables, was the most important \nyardstick of single-year managerial performance. While we still \nbelieve this to be the case with the vast majority of companies, \nwe believe its utility in our own case has greatly diminished. \nYou should be suspicious of such an assertion. Yardsticks seldom \nare discarded while yielding favorable readings. But when \nresults deteriorate, most managers favor disposition of the \nyardstick rather than disposition of the manager.\n\n To managers faced with such deterioration, a more flexible \nmeasurement system often suggests itself: just shoot the arrow of \nbusiness performance into a blank canvas and then carefully draw \nthe bullseye around the implanted arrow. We generally believe in \npre-set, long-lived and small bullseyes. However, because of the \nimportance of item (3) above, further explained in the following \nsection, we believe our abandonment of the operating \nearnings/equity capital bullseye to be warranted.\n\nNon-Reported Ownership Earnings\n\n The appended financial statements reflect “accounting” \nearnings that generally include our proportionate share of \nearnings from any underlying business in which our ownership is \nat least 20%. Below the 20% ownership figure, however, only our \nshare of dividends paid by the underlying business units is \nincluded in our accounting numbers; undistributed earnings of \nsuch less-than-20%-owned businesses are totally ignored.\n\n There are a few exceptions to this rule; e.g., we own about \n35% of GEICO Corporation but, because we have assigned our voting \nrights, the company is treated for accounting purposes as a less-\nthan-20% holding. Thus, dividends received from GEICO in 1982 of \n$3.5 million after tax are the only item included in our \n“accounting”earnings. An additional $23 million that represents \nour share of GEICO’s undistributed operating earnings for 1982 is \ntotally excluded from our reported operating earnings. If GEICO \nhad earned less money in 1982 but had paid an additional $1 \nmillion in dividends, our reported earnings would have been \nlarger despite the poorer business results. Conversely, if GEICO \nhad earned an additional $100 million - and retained it all - our \nreported earnings would have been unchanged. Clearly \n“accounting” earnings can seriously misrepresent economic \nreality.\n\n We prefer a concept of “economic” earnings that includes all \nundistributed earnings, regardless of ownership percentage. In \nour view, the value to all owners of the retained earnings of a \nbusiness enterprise is determined by the effectiveness with which \nthose earnings are used - and not by the size of one’s ownership \npercentage. If you have owned .01 of 1% of Berkshire during the \npast decade, you have benefited economically in full measure from \nyour share of our retained earnings, no matter what your \naccounting system. Proportionately, you have done just as well \nas if you had owned the magic 20%. But if you have owned 100% of \na great many capital-intensive businesses during the decade, \nretained earnings that were credited fully and with painstaking \nprecision to you under standard accounting methods have resulted \nin minor or zero economic value. This is not a criticism of \naccounting procedures. We would not like to have the job of \ndesigning a better system. It’s simply to say that managers and \ninvestors alike must understand that accounting numbers are the \nbeginning, not the end, of business valuation.\n\n In most corporations, less-than-20% ownership positions are \nunimportant (perhaps, in part, because they prevent maximization \nof cherished reported earnings) and the distinction between \naccounting and economic results we have just discussed matters \nlittle. But in our own case, such positions are of very large \nand growing importance. Their magnitude, we believe, is what \nmakes our reported operating earnings figure of limited \nsignificance.\n\n In our 1981 annual report we predicted that our share of \nundistributed earnings from four of our major non-controlled \nholdings would aggregate over $35 million in 1982. With no \nchange in our holdings of three of these companies - GEICO, \nGeneral Foods and The Washington Post - and a considerable \nincrease in our ownership of the fourth, R. J. Reynolds \nIndustries, our share of undistributed 1982 operating earnings of \nthis group came to well over $40 million. This number - not \nreflected at all in our earnings - is greater than our total \nreported earnings, which include only the $14 million in \ndividends received from these companies. And, of course, we have \na number of smaller ownership interests that, in aggregate, had \nsubstantial additional undistributed earnings.\n\n We attach real significance to the general magnitude of \nthese numbers, but we don’t believe they should be carried to ten \ndecimal places. Realization by Berkshire of such retained \nearnings through improved market valuations is subject to very \nsubstantial, but indeterminate, taxation. And while retained \nearnings over the years, and in the aggregate, have translated \ninto at least equal market value for shareholders, the \ntranslation has been both extraordinarily uneven among companies \nand irregular and unpredictable in timing.\n\n However, this very unevenness and irregularity offers \nadvantages to the value-oriented purchaser of fractional portions \nof businesses. This investor may select from almost the entire \narray of major American corporations, including many far superior \nto virtually any of the businesses that could be bought in their \nentirety in a negotiated deal. And fractional-interest purchases \ncan be made in an auction market where prices are set by \nparticipants with behavior patterns that sometimes resemble those \nof an army of manic-depressive lemmings.\n\n Within this gigantic auction arena, it is our job to select \nbusinesses with economic characteristics allowing each dollar of \nretained earnings to be translated eventually into at least a \ndollar of market value. Despite a lot of mistakes, we have so \nfar achieved this goal. In doing so, we have been greatly \nassisted by Arthur Okun’s patron saint for economists - St. \nOffset. In some cases, that is, retained earnings attributable \nto our ownership position have had insignificant or even negative \nimpact on market value, while in other major positions a dollar \nretained by an investee corporation has been translated into two \nor more dollars of market value. To date, our corporate over-\nachievers have more than offset the laggards. If we can continue \nthis record, it will validate our efforts to maximize “economic” \nearnings, regardless of the impact upon “accounting” earnings.\n\n Satisfactory as our partial-ownership approach has been, \nwhat really makes us dance is the purchase of 100% of good \nbusinesses at reasonable prices. We’ve accomplished this feat a \nfew times (and expect to do so again), but it is an \nextraordinarily difficult job - far more difficult than the \npurchase at attractive prices of fractional interests.\n\n As we look at the major acquisitions that others made during \n1982, our reaction is not envy, but relief that we were non-\nparticipants. For in many of these acquisitions, managerial \nintellect wilted in competition with managerial adrenaline The \nthrill of the chase blinded the pursuers to the consequences of \nthe catch. Pascal’s observation seems apt: “It has struck me \nthat all men’s misfortunes spring from the single cause that they \nare unable to stay quietly in one room.”\n\n (Your Chairman left the room once too often last year and \nalmost starred in the Acquisition Follies of 1982. In \nretrospect, our major accomplishment of the year was that a very \nlarge purchase to which we had firmly committed was unable to be \ncompleted for reasons totally beyond our control. Had it come \noff, this transaction would have consumed extraordinary amounts \nof time and energy, all for a most uncertain payoff. If we were \nto introduce graphics to this report, illustrating favorable \nbusiness developments of the past year, two blank pages depicting \nthis blown deal would be the appropriate centerfold.)\n\n Our partial-ownership approach can be continued soundly only \nas long as portions of attractive businesses can be acquired at \nattractive prices. We need a moderately-priced stock market to \nassist us in this endeavor. The market, like the Lord, helps \nthose who help themselves. But, unlike the Lord, the market does \nnot forgive those who know not what they do. For the investor, a \ntoo-high purchase price for the stock of an excellent company can \nundo the effects of a subsequent decade of favorable business \ndevelopments.\n\n Should the stock market advance to considerably higher \nlevels, our ability to utilize capital effectively in partial-\nownership positions will be reduced or eliminated. This will \nhappen periodically: just ten years ago, at the height of the \ntwo-tier market mania (with high-return-on-equity businesses bid \nto the sky by institutional investors), Berkshire’s insurance \nsubsidiaries owned only $18 million in market value of equities, \nexcluding their interest in Blue Chip Stamps. At that time, such \nequity holdings amounted to about 15% of our insurance company \ninvestments versus the present 80%. There were as many good \nbusinesses around in 1972 as in 1982, but the prices the stock \nmarket placed upon those businesses in 1972 looked absurd. While \nhigh stock prices in the future would make our performance look \ngood temporarily, they would hurt our long-term business \nprospects rather than help them. We currently are seeing early \ntraces of this problem.\n\nLong-Term Corporate Performance\n\n Our gain in net worth during 1982, valuing equities held by \nour insurance subsidiaries at market value (less capital gain \ntaxes payable if unrealized gains were actually realized) \namounted to $208 million. On a beginning net worth base of $519 \nmillion, the percentage gain was 40%.\n\n During the 18-year tenure of present management, book value \nhas grown from $19.46 per share to $737.43 per share, or 22.0% \ncompounded annually. You can be certain that this percentage \nwill diminish in the future. Geometric progressions eventually \nforge their own anchors.\n\n Berkshire’s economic goal remains to produce a long-term \nrate of return well above the return achieved by the average \nlarge American corporation. Our willingness to purchase either \npartial or total ownership positions in favorably-situated \nbusinesses, coupled with reasonable discipline about the prices \nwe are willing to pay, should give us a good chance of achieving \nour goal.\n\n Again this year the gain in market valuation of partially-\nowned businesses outpaced the gain in underlying economic value \nof those businesses. For example, $79 million of our $208 \nmillion gain is attributable to an increased market price for \nGEICO. This company continues to do exceptionally well, and we \nare more impressed than ever by the strength of GEICO’s basic \nbusiness idea and by the management skills of Jack Byrne. \n(Although not found in the catechism of the better business \nschools, “Let Jack Do It” works fine as a corporate creed for \nus.)\n\n However, GEICO’s increase in market value during the past \ntwo years has been considerably greater than the gain in its \nintrinsic business value, impressive as the latter has been. We \nexpected such a favorable variation at some point, as the \nperception of investors converged with business reality. And we \nlook forward to substantial future gains in underlying business \nvalue accompanied by irregular, but eventually full, market \nrecognition of such gains.\n\n Year-to-year variances, however, cannot consistently be in \nour favor. Even if our partially-owned businesses continue to \nperform well in an economic sense, there will be years when they \nperform poorly in the market. At such times our net worth could \nshrink significantly. We will not be distressed by such a \nshrinkage; if the businesses continue to look attractive and we \nhave cash available, we simply will add to our holdings at even \nmore favorable prices.\n\nSources of Reported Earnings\n\n The table below shows the sources of Berkshire’s reported \nearnings. In insurance, as \nelsewhere, the reaction of weak managements to weak operations is \noften weak accounting. (“It’s difficult for an empty sack to \nstand upright.”)\n\n The great majority of managements, however, try to play it \nstraight. But even managements of integrity may subconsciously \nbe less willing in poor profit years to fully recognize adverse \nloss trends. Industry statistics indicate some deterioration in \nloss reserving practices during 1982 and the true combined ratio \nis likely to be modestly worse than indicated by our table.\n\n The conventional wisdom is that 1983 or 1984 will see the \nworst of underwriting experience and then, as in the past, the \n“cycle” will move, significantly and steadily, toward better \nresults. We disagree because of a pronounced change in the \ncompetitive environment, hard to see for many years but now quite \nvisible.\n\n To understand the change, we need to look at some major \nfactors that affect levels of corporate profitability generally. \nBusinesses in industries with both substantial over-capacity and \na “commodity” product (undifferentiated in any customer-important \nway by factors such as performance, appearance, service support, \netc.) are prime candidates for profit troubles. These may be \nescaped, true, if prices or costs are administered in some manner \nand thereby insulated at least partially from normal market \nforces. This administration can be carried out (a) legally \nthrough government intervention (until recently, this category \nincluded pricing for truckers and deposit costs for financial \ninstitutions), (b) illegally through collusion, or (c) “extra-\nlegally” through OPEC-style foreign cartelization (with tag-along \nbenefits for domestic non-cartel operators).\n\n If, however, costs and prices are determined by full-bore \ncompetition, there is more than ample capacity, and the buyer \ncares little about whose product or distribution services he \nuses, industry economics are almost certain to be unexciting. \nThey may well be disastrous.\n\n Hence the constant struggle of every vendor to establish and \nemphasize special qualities of product or service. This works \nwith candy bars (customers buy by brand name, not by asking for a \n“two-ounce candy bar”) but doesn’t work with sugar (how often do \nyou hear, “I’ll have a cup of coffee with cream and C \u0026 H sugar, \nplease”).\n\n In many industries, differentiation simply can’t be made \nmeaningful. A few producers in such industries may consistently \ndo well if they have a cost advantage that is both wide and \nsustainable. By definition such exceptions are few, and, in many \nindustries, are non-existent. For the great majority of \ncompanies selling “commodity”products, a depressing equation of \nbusiness economics prevails: persistent over-capacity without \nadministered prices (or costs) equals poor profitability.\n\n Of course, over-capacity may eventually self-correct, either \nas capacity shrinks or demand expands. Unfortunately for the \nparticipants, such corrections often are long delayed. When they \nfinally occur, the rebound to prosperity frequently produces a \npervasive enthusiasm for expansion that, within a few years, \nagain creates over-capacity and a new profitless environment. In \nother words, nothing fails like success.\n\n What finally determines levels of long-term profitability in \nsuch industries is the ratio of supply-tight to supply-ample \nyears. Frequently that ratio is dismal. (It seems as if the most \nrecent supply-tight period in our textile business - it occurred \nsome years back - lasted the better part of a morning.)\n\n In some industries, however, capacity-tight conditions can \nlast a long time. Sometimes actual growth in demand will outrun \nforecasted growth for an extended period. In other cases, adding \ncapacity requires very long lead times because complicated \nmanufacturing facilities must be planned and built.\n\n But in the insurance business, to return to that subject, \ncapacity can be instantly created by capital plus an \nunderwriter’s willingness to sign his name. (Even capital is less \nimportant in a world in which state-sponsored guaranty funds \nprotect many policyholders against insurer insolvency.) Under \nalmost all conditions except that of fear for survival - \nproduced, perhaps, by a stock market debacle or a truly major \nnatural disaster - the insurance industry operates under the \ncompetitive sword of substantial overcapacity. Generally, also, \ndespite heroic attempts to do otherwise, the industry sells a \nrelatively undifferentiated commodity-type product. (Many \ninsureds, including the managers of large businesses, do not even \nknow the names of their insurers.) Insurance, therefore, would \nseem to be a textbook case of an industry usually faced with the \ndeadly combination of excess capacity and a “commodity” product.\n\n Why, then, was underwriting, despite the existence of \ncycles, generally profitable over many decades? (From 1950 \nthrough 1970, the industry combined ratio averaged 99.0. \nallowing all investment income plus 1% of premiums to flow \nthrough to profits.) The answer lies primarily in the historic \nmethods of regulation and distribution. For much of this \ncentury, a large portion of the industry worked, in effect, \nwithin a legal quasi-administered pricing system fostered by \ninsurance regulators. While price competition existed, it was \nnot pervasive among the larger companies. The main competition \nwas for agents, who were courted via various non-price-related \nstrategies.\n\n For the giants of the industry, most rates were set through \nnegotiations between industry “bureaus” (or through companies \nacting in accord with their recommendations) and state \nregulators. Dignified haggling occurred, but it was between \ncompany and regulator rather than between company and customer. \nWhen the dust settled, Giant A charged the same price as Giant B \n- and both companies and agents were prohibited by law from \ncutting such filed rates.\n\n The company-state negotiated prices included specific profit \nallowances and, when loss data indicated that current prices were \nunprofitable, both company managements and state regulators \nexpected that they would act together to correct the situation. \nThus, most of the pricing actions of the giants of the industry \nwere “gentlemanly”, predictable, and profit-producing. Of prime \nimportance - and in contrast to the way most of the business \nworld operated - insurance companies could legally price their \nway to profitability even in the face of substantial over-\ncapacity.\n\n That day is gone. Although parts of the old structure \nremain, far more than enough new capacity exists outside of that \nstructure to force all parties, old and new, to respond. The new \ncapacity uses various methods of distribution and is not \nreluctant to use price as a prime competitive weapon. Indeed, it \nrelishes that use. In the process, customers have learned that \ninsurance is no longer a one-price business. They won’t forget.\n\n Future profitability of the industry will be determined by \ncurrent competitive characteristics, not past ones. Many \nmanagers have been slow to recognize this. It’s not only \ngenerals that prefer to fight the last war. Most business and \ninvestment analysis also comes from the rear-view mirror. It \nseems clear to us, however, that only one condition will allow \nthe insurance industry to achieve significantly improved \nunderwriting results. That is the same condition that will allow \nbetter results for the aluminum, copper, or corn producer - a \nmajor narrowing of the gap between demand and supply.\n\n Unfortunately, there can be no surge in demand for insurance \npolicies comparable to one that might produce a market tightness \nin copper or aluminum. Rather, the supply of available insurance \ncoverage must be curtailed. “Supply”, in this context, is mental \nrather than physical: plants or companies need not be shut; only \nthe willingness of underwriters to sign their names need be \ncurtailed.\n\n This contraction will not happen because of generally poor \nprofit levels. Bad profits produce much hand-wringing and \nfinger-pointing. But they do not lead major sources of insurance \ncapacity to turn their backs on very large chunks of business, \nthereby sacrificing market share and industry significance.\n\n Instead, major capacity withdrawals require a shock factor \nsuch as a natural or financial “megadisaster”. One might occur \ntomorrow - or many years from now. The insurance business - even \ntaking investment income into account - will not be particularly \nprofitable in the meantime.\n\n When supply ultimately contracts, large amounts of business \nwill be available for the few with large capital capacity, a \nwillingness to commit it, and an in-place distribution system. \nWe would expect great opportunities for our insurance \nsubsidiaries at such a time.\n\n During 1982, our insurance underwriting deteriorated far \nmore than did the industry’s. From a profit position well above \naverage, we, slipped to a performance modestly below average. \nThe biggest swing was in National Indemnity’s traditional \ncoverages.
