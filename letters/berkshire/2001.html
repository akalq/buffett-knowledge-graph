BERKSHIRE HATHAWAY INC. To the Shareholders of Berkshire Hathaway Inc.: Berkshire's loss in net worth during 2001 was $3.77 billion, which decreased the per-share book value of both our Class A and Class B stock by 6.2%. Over the last 37 years (that is, since present management took over) per-share book value has grown from $19 to $37,920, a rate of 22.6% compounded annually. Per-share intrinsic value grew somewhat faster than book value during these 37 years, and in 2001 it probably decreased a bit less. We explain intrinsic value in our Owner's Manual, which begins on page 62. I urge new shareholders to read this manual to become familiar with Berkshire's key economic principles. Two years ago, reporting on 1999, I said that we had experienced both the worst absolute and relative performance in our history. I added that "relative results are what concern us," a viewpoint I've had since forming my first investment partnership on May 5, 1956. Meeting with my seven founding limited partners that evening, I gave them a short paper titled "The Ground Rules" that included this sentence: "Whether we do a good job or a poor job is to be measured against the general experience in securities." We initially used the Dow Jones Industrials as our benchmark, but shifted to the S&P 500 when that index became widely used. Our comparative record since 1965 is chronicled on the facing page; last year Berkshire's advantage was 5.7 percentage points. Some people disagree with our focus on relative figures, arguing that "you can't eat relative performance." But if you expect – as Charlie Munger, Berkshire's Vice Chairman, and I do – that owning the S&P 500 will produce reasonably satisfactory results over time, it follows that, for long-term investors, gaining small advantages annually over that index must prove rewarding. Just as you can eat well throughout the year if you own a profitable, but highly seasonal, business such as See's (which loses considerable money during the summer months) so, too, can you regularly feast on investment returns that beat the averages, however variable the absolute numbers may be. Though our corporate performance last year was satisfactory, my performance was anything but. I manage most of Berkshire's equity portfolio, and my results were poor, just as they have been for several years. Of even more importance, I allowed General Re to take on business without a safeguard I knew was important, and on September 11th, this error caught up with us. I'll tell you more about my mistake later and what we are doing to correct it.

Acquisitions of 2001 A few days before last year's annual meeting, I received a heavy package from St. Louis, containing an unprepossessing chunk of metal whose function I couldn't imagine. It took me only a minute to realize that he was our kind of manager and MiTek our kind of business. We made a cash offer to the U.K. owner and before long had a deal. Gene's managerial crew is exceptionally enthusiastic about the company and wanted to participate in the purchase. Therefore, we arranged for 55 members of the MiTek team to buy 10% of the company, with each putting up a minimum of $100,000 in cash. Many borrowed money so they could participate. As they would not be if they had options, all of these managers are true owners. They face the downside of decisions as well as the upside. They incur a cost of capital. And they can't "reprice" their stakes: What they paid is what they live with. Charlie and I love the high-grade, truly entrepreneurial attitude that exists at MiTek, and we predict it will be a winner for all involved. And interestingly – unlike the situation prevailing in many other industries – neither size nor brand name determines an insurer's profitability. "Loss Development" and Insurance Accounting Bad terminology is the enemy of good thinking. When companies or investment professionals use terms such as "EBITDA" and "pro forma," they want you to unthinkingly accept concepts that are dangerously flawed. In insurance reporting, "loss development" is a widely used term – and one that is seriously misleading. First, a definition: Loss reserves at an insurer are not funds tucked away for a rainy day, but rather a liability account. If properly calculated, the liability states the amount that an insurer will have to pay for all losses (including associated costs) that have occurred prior to the reporting date but have not yet been paid. When calculating the reserve, the insurer will have been notified of many of the losses it is destined to pay, but others will not yet have been reported to it. These losses are called IBNR, for incurred but not reported. It's clearly difficult for an insurer to put a figure on the ultimate cost of all such reported and unreported events. But the ability to do so with reasonable accuracy is vital. Otherwise the insurer's managers won't know what its actual loss costs are and how these compare to the premiums being charged. GEICO got into huge trouble in the early 1970s because for several years it severely underreserved, and therefore believed its product (insurance protection) was costing considerably less than was truly the case. When it becomes evident that reserves at past reporting dates understated the liability that truly existed at the time, companies speak of "loss development." In the year discovered, these shortfalls penalize reported earnings because the "catch-up" costs from prior years must be added to current-year costs when results are calculated. This is what happened at General Re in 2001: a staggering $800 million of loss costs that actually occurred in earlier years, but that were not then recorded, were belatedly recognized last year and charged against current earnings. The mistake was an honest one, I can assure you of that. Additionally, the overstated profit figures led us to pay substantial incentive compensation that we should not have and to incur income taxes far earlier than was necessary. We recommend scrapping the term "loss development" and its equally ugly twin, "reserve strengthening." "Loss development" suggests to investors that some natural, uncontrollable event has occurred in the current year, and "reserve strengthening" implies that adequate amounts have been further buttressed. 年度会议 今年的年会将于5月4日（周六）举行，我们将在市民礼堂再次举行。门将于上午7点开放，电影将于8:30开始，会议本身将于9:30开始。中午会有短暂的休息时间提供食物。除休息时间外，查理和我将回答问题至下午3:30。向我们抛出你的最佳问题吧。 至少在明年，位于市中心的市民礼堂是我们唯一可用的场所。因此，我们必须将会议安排在周六或周日，以避免工作日肯定会发生的交通和停车噩梦。然而，很快奥马哈将有一个新的会议中心，拥有充足的停车设施。如果我们去会议中心，我将征求股东意见，看你们是否希望恢复2000年之前惯例的周一会议。我们将根据股东数量而不是股份数量来决定投票。 委托书材料中有一个附件，解释了如何获得参加会议和其他活动所需的凭证。至于机票、酒店和租车预订，我们再次与American Express（800-799-6634）签约，让他们为您提供特别帮助。他们每年为我们做的工作都非常出色。 按照惯例，我们将从较大的酒店到会议现场运行巴士。会议结束后，巴士将返回酒店、内布拉斯加家具市场、博瑟姆珠宝店和机场。尽管如此，你可能还是会发现租车很有用。今年我们为伯克希尔增加了这么多新公司，我不打算详细说明我们将在会议上销售的所有产品。但要做好从砖块到糖果的一切准备。当然还有内衣。 假设我们在5月4日之前完成了Fruit of the Loom的收购，我们将销售Fruit的最新款式，这将使你成为你所在社区的时尚领袖。买够穿一辈子的量吧。 GEICO将有一个展位，由来自全国各地的多名顶级顾问组成，他们都可以为您提供汽车保险报价。在大多数情况下，GEICO将能够为您提供特别的股东折扣（通常为8%）。这一特别优惠在我们运营的49个管辖区中的41个是允许的。 在内布拉斯加家具市场（位于72街和太平洋之间的75英亩土地上），我们将再次实行"伯克希尔周末"价格，这意味着我们将向股东提供通常仅向员工提供的折扣。我们五年前在NFM启动了这一特别价格，"周末"期间的销售额从1997年的530万美元增长到2001年的1150万美元。要获得折扣，你必须在5月2日周四至5月6日周一期间购物，并出示你的会议凭证。 博瑟姆珠宝店——全国最大的珠宝店，仅次于蒂芙尼的曼哈顿店——将举办两场仅限股东参加的活动。第一场是5月3日（周五）下午6点至10点的鸡尾酒会。第二场是主要庆典，将于5月5日（周日）上午9点至下午5点举行。周四至周一都可享受股东价格，所以如果你想避开周五晚上和周日的大量人群，请在其他时间前来，并出示你的股东身份。 在博瑟姆外面的商场里，我们会有一些世界顶级桥牌专家在周日下午与我们的股东对弈。我们期待鲍勃和佩特拉·哈曼以及沙龙·奥斯伯格主持牌桌。两次获得美国象棋冠军的帕特里克·沃尔夫也将在商场里，接受所有人的挑战——蒙眼！去年，帕特里克同时下了多达六局棋——他的眼罩牢牢地戴好了——今年将尝试七局。 最后，Bill Robertie——仅有的两位两次获得双陆棋世界冠军的选手之一——将在场测试你在那个游戏中的技能。周日来商场参加门萨奥运会吧。 Gorat's——我最喜欢的牛排馆——将再次仅向伯克希尔股东开放，时间是5月5日（周日）下午4点至晚上10点。请记住，周日去Gorat's你必须提前预订。要预订，请在4月1日（但不早于那天）拨打402-551-3733。如果周日已满座，在你逗留的其他晚上尝试去Gorat's。点一份三分熟的T骨牛排配双份薯饼，展示你的品位。 通常的棒球比赛将于周六晚上7点在罗森布拉特体育场举行。今年奥马哈皇家队将对阵俄克拉荷马红鹰队。去年，为了模仿贝比·鲁斯的职业生涯转型，我放弃投球，尝试击球。奥马哈本地人鲍勃·吉布森站在投手丘上，我很害怕，担心鲍勃著名的触身球。然而，他在好球区投了一个快球，我用类似马克·麦格威尔的挥棒姿势，成功打出了一个强劲的滚地球，却莫名其妙地死在内场。我没有跑垒：以我的年龄，打一手桥牌就会气喘吁吁。我不确定今年棒球场会发生什么，但出来看看，会有惊喜的。 我们的委托书包含获取比赛门票的说明。订购年度会议门票的人将收到一本小册子，其中包含各种信息，应该能帮助你享受奥马哈之行。城里会有很多活动。来参加伍德斯托克周末，来市民礼堂参加我们的资本主义庆典吧。 最后，我想感谢世界总部那群出色且工作效率极高的团队（总共5246.5平方英尺），是他们让我的工作变得轻松。去年伯克希尔增加了约4万名员工，使我们的员工总数达到11万人。在总部，我们增加了一名员工，现在共有14.8人。（我徒劳地试图说服乔埃伦·里克将她的工作周从四天改为五天；我认为她喜欢因工作0.8天而获得的国家认可。）处理我们当前规模和范围所带来的各种职责——以及一些几乎是伯克希尔独有的额外活动，如股东庆典和指定捐赠计划——需要一群非常特殊的人。而这正是我们绝对拥有的。
